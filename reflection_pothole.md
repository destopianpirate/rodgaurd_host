# Individual Reflection: RoadGuard Pothole Detection Project

**Project Name**: RoadGuard — Advanced Pothole Detection & Road Safety System
**Student Perspective**: Draft for Personal Reflection (approx. 270 words)

At the start of this course, my first ideation assignment focused on a singular, almost simplistic goal: using a camera to spot road damage. Back then, my perspective as an engineer was largely theoretical, viewing the problem as a straightforward data challenge. However, as the prototype for RoadGuard has reached its current stage—a complex, multi-threaded system integrated with GPS, IMU vibrations, and real-time dashboarding—my perspective has evolved significantly. I’ve realized that engineering is less about the "ideal case" and more about the "robust case."

What excited me most was the first time we saw a GPS marker pop up on the web dashboard in real-time. Seeing a physical location translated instantly into a digital hazard map felt like bringing a concept to life. Conversely, what frightened me was the unpredictable nature of hardware. Specifically, the frequent system crashes on the Raspberry Pi due to thermal throttling and camera initialization failures during early testing were deeply unsettling. It taught me that software brilliance means nothing if the hardware environment isn't resilient.

My understanding of design and prototyping has shifted from a linear process to a cyclical one. I now see that prototyping is a dialogue with constraints—network latency, GPS "no fix" errors, and power requirements. My single biggest takeaway from this course is that engineering is 20% about solving the core problem and 80% about handling the "what-ifs." Building a prototype that works on a desk is easy; building one that survives a moving vehicle is where real engineering begins. I have learned to prioritize reliable system integration over isolated feature complexity.
